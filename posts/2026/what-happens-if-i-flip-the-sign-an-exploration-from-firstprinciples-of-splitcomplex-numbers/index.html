I have been brushing up on my abstract algebra lately. At the moment I am reading Linear Algebra Done Right and Aluffi’s Algebra: Notes from the Underground. To practice the ideas, I decided, at least for a while, not to do the book exercises. Instead, I wanted to experiment on my own—not to discover anything new, but to make the discovery myself, slowly enough to get to know the resulting structure from the inside.

I started with a very small question. We all learn early on that the complex numbers arise by adjoining a formal symbol \(i\) with the rule

\[ i^2=-1. \]

So I wondered what would happen if I made one tiny change to that story. What if I adjoined a new symbol \(t\) with the rule

\[ t^2=1? \]

At first this looked like a harmless variation. It turned out not to be harmless at all. That one sign change led me, step by step, into the split-complex numbers, then into orthogonal idempotents, then into function algebras on finite sets, and finally into the subtler infinite-dimensional story where indicator functions behave like multiplicative atoms and, under the right hypotheses, even become an honest orthonormal basis.

What follows is that path of discovery, cleaned up from my scratch work but still written in the order in which the structure gradually revealed itself.

1. Starting from first principles: adjoining \(t\) with \(t^2=1\)

Take all formal expressions of the form

\[ x=u+vt, \]

where \(u,v \in\mathbb R\), and define addition and multiplication exactly as one would expect, using distributivity together with the single rule \(t^2=1\).

If

\[ x=u+vt, \qquad y=a+bt, \]

then

\[ x+y=(u+a)+(v+b)t \]

and

\[ xy=(u+vt)(a+bt)=ua+ubt+vat+vbt^2=(ua+vb)+(ub+va)t. \]

So this gives a \(2\)-dimensional real algebra. It looks suspiciously like the complex numbers, except that the basic quadratic relation has the opposite sign. In \(\mathbb C\) we impose \(i^2=-1\); here we are imposing \(t^2=1\).

That tiny formal change turns out to alter the structure completely.

2. Zero divisors appear immediately

The first test multiplication is the obvious one:

\[ (1+t)(1-t)=1-t^2=0. \]

So two nonzero elements multiply to zero.

That already marks a decisive break from the complex numbers. In \(\mathbb C\), nonzero elements never annihilate one another. Here they do, immediately. So whatever this algebra is, it is not a field. It is doing something more decomposed—something split.

And once the factors \(1+t\) and \(1-t\) appear, it is hard not to divide them by \(2\) and see what happens. Define

\[ e_+=\frac{1+t}{2}, \qquad e_- = \frac{1-t}{2}. \]

Now compute:

\[ e_+^2=\frac{(1+t)^2}{4}=\frac{1+2t+t^2}{4}=\frac{2+2t}{4}=\frac{1+t}{2}=e_+, \]

and similarly,

\[ e_-^2=e_-. \]

Also,

\[ e_+e_- = \frac{(1+t)(1-t)}{4}=0. \]

And finally,

\[ e_++e_-=1, \qquad e_+-e_-=t. \]

This is the moment when the algebra suddenly becomes transparent. The new basis elements are

  • idempotent: \(e_\pm^2=e_\pm\),
  • orthogonal under multiplication: \(e_+e_-=0\),
  • and they sum to the identity: \(e_++e_-=1\).

I had started with a naive sign-flip experiment, and now a remarkably rigid structure had appeared almost uninvited.

3. Every element rewrites naturally in the idempotent basis

Since

\[ 1=e_+ + e_-, \qquad t=e_+ - e_-, \]

any element

\[ x=u+vt \]

can be rewritten as

\[ x=u(e_++e_-)+v(e_+-e_-) =(u+v)e_+ + (u-v)e_-. \]

So if I define

\[ a=u+v, \qquad b=u-v, \]

then every element takes the form

\[ x=ae_+ + be_-. \]

This already suggests that the algebra is decomposing into two independent channels.

And now multiplication diagonalizes completely. If

\[ x=ae_+ + be_-, \qquad y=ce_+ + de_-, \]

then, using only

\[ e_+^2=e_+, \qquad e_-^2=e_-, \qquad e_+e_-=0, \]

we get

\[ xy=(ae_+ + be_-)(ce_+ + de_-)=ace_+ + bde_-. \]

So in these coordinates, addition and multiplication become

\[ (a,b)+(c,d)=(a+c,b+d) \]

and

4. What the algebra actually is

At this point the abstract description becomes recognizable. The algebra I built is

\[ \mathbb R[t]/(t^2-1). \]

Since

\[ t^2-1=(t-1)(t+1), \]

and the two factors are coprime, the Chinese remainder theorem gives

\[ \mathbb R[t]/(t^2-1) \cong \mathbb R[t]/(t-1)\times \mathbb R[t]/(t+1) \cong \mathbb R\times\mathbb R. \]

Concretely, the isomorphism is

\[ u+vt \longmapsto (u+v,u-v). \]

So this strange-looking algebra is not strange at all once the right coordinates are chosen. It is simply \(\mathbb R^2\) with coordinatewise multiplication.

As I learned after doing the whole exploration, this is the classical algebra usually called the split-complex numbers, though it also goes by names such as the hyperbolic numbers, double numbers, or perplex numbers.

So the experiment had not produced an exotic new object at all. It had backed, almost accidentally, into a classical one. That only made me want to keep going.

Inverses and zero divisors

In the \((a,b)\) coordinates, inversion is immediate. The element \((a,b)\) is invertible exactly when both coordinates are nonzero, and in that case

\[ (a,b)^{-1}=\left(\frac1a,\frac1b\right). \]

Translating this back into the \(u+vt\) form gives

\[ (u+vt)^{-1}=\frac{u-vt}{u^2-v^2}, \]

provided that

\[ u^2-v^2\neq 0. \]

So the zero divisors are precisely the elements satisfying

\[ u^2-v^2=0, \]

that is,

\[ u=v \qquad \text{or} \qquad u=-v. \]

Geometrically, in the \((u,v)\)-plane, the singular set is the union of the two lines

\[ u=v \qquad \text{and} \qquad u=-v. \]

This is one of the places where the familiar hyperbolic geometry of the split-complex numbers begins to peek through.

Functions act coordinatewise

Once the idempotent basis is in place, another pleasant fact becomes almost automatic. For any polynomial \(p\), one has

\[ p(a\operatorname{e}_+ + b \operatorname{e}_-)=p(a)\operatorname{e}_+ + p(b)\operatorname{e}_-. \]

The reason is simple: powers never mix,

\[ (a \operatorname e_+ + b \operatorname e_-)^n = a^n \operatorname e_+ + b^n \operatorname e_-, \]

because all cross-terms die. And whenever a function defined by a power-series converges, the same pointwise rule continues to hold.

So, for example,

\[ \exp{(a \operatorname e_+ +\, b \operatorname e_-)}=\exp(a) \operatorname e_+ +\, \exp(b) \operatorname e_-. \]

Returning to the \(u+vt\) coordinates, where \(a=u+v\) and \(b=u-v\), this becomes

\[ \begin{aligned} e^{u+vt} &=e^{u+v} \operatorname e_+ + e^{u-v} \operatorname e_- \\ &=e^u\bigl(e^v \operatorname e_+ + e^{-v} \operatorname e_-\bigr) \\ &=e^u\left(\cosh v + t\sinh v\right). \end{aligned} \]

This is the hyperbolic analogue of the familiar complex formula

\[ e^{x+yi}=e^x(\cos y + i\sin y). \]

And the idempotents do something else neat as well: they isolate the two coordinates. If

\[ x=a \operatorname e_+ + b \operatorname e_-, \]

then

\[ \operatorname e_+x=a \operatorname e_+, \qquad \operatorname e_-x= b \operatorname e_-. \]

Likewise, for any coordinatewise-defined function \(f\),

\[ \operatorname e_+f(a,b)=f(a) \operatorname e_+, \qquad \operatorname e_-f(a,b)=f(b) \operatorname e_-. \]

So the idempotents are not just basis vectors. They are also projection operators onto the two independent channels.

5. The right conceptual name: complete orthogonal idempotents

The pair \(e_+,e_-\) is not just a lucky trick. It belongs to a general pattern.

A family \(e_1,\dots,e_n\) in an algebra is called a complete set of pairwise orthogonal idempotents if

\[ e_i^2=e_i, \qquad e_ie_j=0 \text{ for } i\neq j, \qquad e_1+\cdots+e_n=1. \]

That is exactly what happened here.

This is not the same thing as an orthonormal basis in an inner-product space, because the orthogonality here is multiplicative rather than metric. But the analogy is strong. An orthonormal basis diagonalizes an inner product; a complete set of orthogonal idempotents diagonalizes multiplication.

The formulas even rhyme:

\[ \langle v_i,v_j\rangle = \delta_{ij} \]

versus

\[ e_ie_j=\delta_{ij}e_i. \]

That was one of the most satisfying moments in the exploration: this basis felt like an orthogonal basis, but for algebra itself.

6. The surprise hiding underneath: this is really a function algebra

The coordinatewise multiplication law suggested a different interpretation. Consider the two-point set

\[ S=\{+,-\}. \]

The algebra of real-valued functions on \(S\) is just

\[ \mathbb R^S \cong \mathbb R\times\mathbb R, \]

with pointwise addition and multiplication.

Now take the two singleton indicators

\[ \delta_+ := \mathbf 1_{\{+\}}, \qquad \delta_- := \mathbf 1_{\{-\}}. \]

They satisfy

\[ \delta_+^2=\delta_+, \qquad \delta_-^2=\delta_-, \]

\[ \delta_+\delta_-=0, \qquad \delta_+ + \delta_- = 1. \]

So the basis \(e_+,e_-\) is doing exactly what the indicator basis does in a two-point function algebra.

This was the point where the experiment took a turn I had not anticipated. It was the first real “oh!” moment for me. I had not merely found a convenient basis for a funny number system. I had stumbled into the algebra of functions on a finite set.

And once that interpretation appears, the general pattern is hard to miss. For any finite set \(S\) and any field \(F\), the algebra

\[ F^S \]

of all \(F\)-valued functions on \(S\) has a canonical basis given by the singleton indicators

\[ \delta_s := \mathbf 1_{\{s\}}, \qquad s\in S. \]

These satisfy

\[ \delta_s^2=\delta_s, \qquad \delta_s\delta_t=0 \text{ for } s\neq t, \qquad \sum_{s\in S}\delta_s=1. \]

Every function expands as

\[ f=\sum_{s\in S} f(s)\,\delta_s, \]

and multiplication is pointwise:

\[ (fg)(s)=f(s)g(s). \]

And so is addition:

\[ (f+g)(s)=f(s)+g(s). \]

So the phenomenon I had rediscovered from the single relation \(t^2=1\) was not a special trick at all. It was the smallest nontrivial example of a general principle:

Function algebras on finite sets come with a canonical multiplicatively orthogonal basis of idempotents.

Even better, the coefficients in that basis are not mysterious. They are just the function values. Multiplying by one of the basis idempotents isolates a single coordinate:

\[ \delta_s f = f(s)\,\delta_s. \]

So the same objects play two roles at once: they are the atomic basis vectors, and they are the algebraic devices that pick out individual coordinates.

In retrospect that makes the original split-complex discovery feel even cleaner. The elements \(e_+\) and \(e_-\) were not merely a convenient change of basis. They were the two point-masses on a two-point space, and multiplication had become pointwise multiplication because the algebra had quietly turned into the algebra of functions on that space.

7. Pushing the original experiment: what if \(t^n=1\)?

At that point it was irresistible to ask for the next step. If adjoining \(t\) with \(t^2=1\) produces a two-point function algebra, what happens if one adjoins a formal symbol satisfying

\[ t^n=1? \]

The resulting algebra is

\[ A_n = F[t]/(t^n-1). \]

Before even factoring the polynomial, one structural feature jumps out. The residue classes

\[ 1,t,t^2,\dots,t^{n-1} \]

multiply according to the rule

\[ t^a t^b = t^{a+b \, \mod n}. \]

So these powers of \(t\) form a cyclic multiplicative group of order \(n\), isomorphic to the additive group \((\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z,+)\). Once linear combinations are allowed, the experiment has quietly moved from a cyclic group to its group algebra. The basis

\[ 1,t,t^2,\dots,t^{n-1} \]

is therefore not just a convenient spanning set. It is the natural basis coming from the group elements themselves.

At first I half-expected some neat \(n\)-fold real splitting. The real answer depends entirely on how the polynomial \(t^n-1\) factors over the base field.

Over \(\mathbb C\)

Over the complex numbers,

\[ t^n-1=\prod_{k=0}^{n-1}(t-\omega^k), \qquad \omega=e^{2\pi i/n}, \]

and the roots are all distinct. So one gets

\[ \mathbb C[t]/(t^n-1)\cong \mathbb C^n. \]

Concretely,

\[ f(t) \longmapsto \bigl(f(1),f(\omega),f(\omega^2),\dots,f(\omega^{n-1})\bigr). \]

So over \(\mathbb C\), the quotient really is the algebra of functions on the finite set of \(n\)th roots of unity.

This was the point where something I had been feeling only vaguely snapped into focus. The roots of unity are not, by themselves, “the basis” of \(\mathbb C^n\). Rather, they index one natural coordinate system: evaluation at the \(n\) points \(1,\omega,\dots,\omega^{n-1}\).

Over \(\mathbb R\)

Over the reals, the picture is only partly split. Real roots contribute one-dimensional real factors, while nonreal roots occur in complex-conjugate pairs and produce quadratic irreducible factors. As real algebras this gives

  • if \(n\) is odd, \[ \mathbb R[t]/(t^n-1)\cong \mathbb R\times \mathbb C^{(n-1)/2}, \]
  • if \(n\) is even, \[ \mathbb R[t]/(t^n-1)\cong \mathbb R\times \mathbb R\times \mathbb C^{(n-2)/2}. \]

So the perfectly real splitting into independent one-dimensional axes is a special luxury of the case \(n=2\).

8. The higher idempotents and the Fourier change of basis

Over \(\mathbb C[t]/(t^n-1)\) there is a direct analogue of the pair \(e_+,e_-\).

Let \(\omega=e^{2\pi i/n}\) and define, for \(j=0,1,\dots,n-1\),

\[ e_j=\frac1n\sum_{m=0}^{n-1} \omega^{-jm} t^m. \]

These are the Fourier idempotents. They satisfy

\[ e_j^2=e_j, \qquad e_je_k=0 \text{ for } j\neq k, \qquad \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} e_j=1. \]

So they form a complete set of orthogonal idempotents.

Under the isomorphism with \(\mathbb C^n\), each \(e_j\) becomes the function that is \(1\) at \(\omega^j\) and \(0\) at all the other roots. In other words, each \(e_j\) is just a singleton indicator in disguise. Once again the algebra has turned into a finite function algebra, and once again the distinguished basis is the atomic one.

But now there is an extra layer of structure. The change of basis is explicit in both directions:

\[ e_j=\frac1n\sum_{m=0}^{n-1} \omega^{-jm} t^m, \]

and conversely,

\[ t^m=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\omega^{jm} e_j. \]

So if \(u=(u_0,\dots,u_{n-1})^\top\) is the coordinate vector in the power basis \(\{1,t,\dots,t^{n-1}\}\) and \(a=(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})^\top\) is the coordinate vector in the idempotent basis \(\{e_0,\dots,e_{n-1}\}\), then

\[ u = Wa, \qquad a=\frac1n\overline{W}\,u, \]

where

\[ W_{mj}=\omega^{mj}. \]

That is exactly the discrete Fourier transform matrix and its inverse, up to the usual normalization. If one rescales by \(1/\sqrt n\), one gets the standard unitary DFT matrix.

This was one of my favorite moments in the whole exploration. I had not just rediscovered a family of orthogonal idempotents. I had backed into the DFT itself: it is the linear change of coordinates between the basis coming from the cyclic group elements and the basis coming from point masses on the corresponding finite function algebra.

There is also a clean group-theoretic way to say the same thing. The powers \(t^m\) are indexed by the cyclic group \(C_n\cong \mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z\). The functions

\[ \chi_m(k)=\omega^{mk} \]

are its characters. The formulas above are precisely the Fourier transform and Fourier inversion for that finite abelian group.

9. One last extension: infinite sets

The same picture survives for an arbitrary, potentially infinite, set \(S\), but now one has to distinguish between a pointwise decomposition and a genuine Hamel-basis expansion. We now ignore the original motivation for \(t^n = 1\) and work directly with the indicators.

Consider the algebra

\[ F^S \]

of all functions \(f:S\to F\).

The singleton indicators still exist:

\[ \delta_s := \mathbf 1_{\{s\}}, \qquad s\in S. \]

They still satisfy

\[ \delta_s^2=\delta_s, \qquad \delta_s\delta_t=0 \text{ for } s\neq t. \]

And every function still has the pointwise decomposition

\[ f(u)=\sum_{s\in S} f(s)\,\delta_s(u) \qquad (u\in S), \]

because at a given point \(u\), exactly one term survives: the one with \(s=u\).

But if \(S\) is infinite, that does not make the family \(\{\delta_s\}_{s\in S}\) a Hamel basis of all of \(F^S\), because Hamel bases allow only finite linear combinations. What we really have is an atomic product decomposition

\[ F^S \cong \prod_{s\in S} F, \]

and the indicator family records those coordinates pointwise.

For the subspace of finitely supported functions,

\[ F^{(S)}, \]

the story does become literally basis-theoretic: the singleton indicators are a genuine basis.

If \(|S|=|\mathbb R| = |\mathbb C| = 2^{\aleph_0}\), one may also choose a bijection

\[ T:\mathbb R \to S \]

and relabel the singleton indicators by real or complex numbers:

\[ \delta_x := \mathbf 1_{\{T(x)\}}, \qquad x\in\mathbb R \text{ or } \mathbb C. \]

That changes only the labels, not the structure. At that stage the role of \(\mathbb R\) is purely as an index set; no topology, order, or measure has entered yet.

10. Under square summability, an honest orthonormal basis

The final refinement appears when one passes from all functions on \(S\) to square-summable ones. With counting measure, this gives the Hilbert space

\[ \ell^2(S). \]

Now the singleton indicators really do form an orthonormal basis:

\[ \langle \delta_s,\delta_t\rangle = \delta_{s,t}, \]

with \(\delta_{s,t}\) the usual Kronecker delta. And every \(f\in \ell^2(S)\) has the expansion

\[ f = \sum_{s\in S} f(s) \, \delta_s \]

with convergence in the \(\ell^2\) norm.

So in this setting the analogy becomes exact. The same family that serves as multiplicative atoms in the algebra \(F^S\) also serves as orthonormal coordinates in the Hilbert space \(\ell^2(S)\).

I liked that as an ending to the exploration. The basis first appeared as an algebraic curiosity inside the split-complex numbers, and only later revealed itself as something that also belongs naturally to Hilbert-space geometry.

11. Looking back

Seen from the end, the thread is surprisingly clean. A single sign flip produced zero divisors; the zero divisors suggested the factors \(1+t\) and \(1-t\); those suggested idempotents; the idempotents diagonalized multiplication; diagonalized multiplication exposed a direct product; and the direct product turned out to be the function algebra on a two-point set. From there the finite-set picture, the cyclic-group generalization, and the Fourier change of basis follow almost inevitably. The infinite case then appears as a final extension, with the same indicator family surviving pointwise and becoming a genuine orthonormal basis once one passes to \(\ell^2(S)\).

So the deeper principle hiding inside the experiment seems to be this:

Whenever an algebra splits into orthogonal idempotent pieces, it begins to behave like a function algebra on a set of mutually independent points.

That is why the basis felt so natural. It was not merely convenient. It was the algebraic analogue of decomposing a space into disjoint atoms.

And that, for me, is one of the pleasures of returning to old mathematics seriously: not just remembering the answer, but watching the answer reveal the structure that was already waiting inside the first small question.

None of this is new mathematics. But starting from the tiny prompt “what if \(t^2=1\)?” and following the consequences by hand made the web of ideas feel unusually vivid to me. That was reason enough to keep going.
