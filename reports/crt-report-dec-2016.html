We have two types of subjects, based on their errors on recombined trials 1) Subjects that more often make an old error - 5, 8, 9, 10 2) Subjects that more often make a new error - 2, 3, 4, 6, 7

plot_percent_response_by_trialtype_subject_fitted(df1)

The bars show the actual data, and the points show the fit of a cumulative-logit model. This is equivalent to an extension of SDT for multinomial responses (http://www.columbia.edu/~ld208/psymeth98.pdf). If we assume that each item causes some familiarity signal, and that there are 2 ordered thresholds, one for responding “recombined” and one for responding “old”, and we estimate the model separately for each pariticipant in which the thresholds are fixed, but the familiarity signal distribution is affected by the trialtype, we get the points above. 6) Type of response as a function of number of previous exemplars of the relations

Here’s the key results - proportion of each response depending on the type of trial (left panel = old trials; right panel = recombined trials), and how many different exemplars of the relation have been presented before the current exemplar.

plot_prop_response_by_trialtype_nexemp(df1)

For old trials, the proportion of old responses increases and the proportion of recombined responses decreases when more exemplars of the relation have been presented. Importantly, participants have seen only 1 time before. The increase in old responses indicates that having seen the relation previously increases the familiarity of the whole pair.

For recombined trials, as predicted people are getting less accurate the more exemplars of the relation they have previously seen. Recombined responses decrease, while old and new responses increase. While the overall result is consistent with our hypothesis, the fact that new responses also increase is not.

Also, we have one confound that can provide an alternative explanation - participants might just be becoming more likely/biased to respond “old” as the experiment progresses, and the “number of previous exemplars” is correlated with the trial number. I ran a multinomial mixed-effects regression model controlling for trial. Below is the same plot after taking into account the effect of trial number.

plot_prop_response_by_trialtype_nexemp_controlling_for_trial(glmer5)

After taking trial into account we can see that the pattern for old trials holds the same, even though the slope is reduced. For recombined trials, the new responses are stable as the number of previous exemplars increase, but the number of old response increase. Thus, not only is the effect of trial not responsible for the main effect of interest, but it was also obscuring it in the original plot. To see the effect even more clearly we can make the three type of response have the same mean intercept, so that we can compare the magnitute of change as the number of exemplars increase:

plot_prop_response_by_trialtype_nexemp_controlling_for_trial_centered(glmer5)

In contrast, here’s the performance that trial number accounts for on its own, while controlling for number of exemplars:

plot_prop_response_by_trialtype_trial_controlling_for_nexemp(glmer5)

In summary, as predicted, as more and more exemplars of a relation have been seen, people respond more often that the item is old, and less often recombined, regardless if the items is old or recombined. This results is not due to increase of bias of trials.

All of these effects are significant as well, even with just 9 people!

Finally, a curious result about memory for items vs memory for the associations for old trials as a function of how many trials ago people saw the previous occurence,

plot_prop_response_by_distance_from_previous_exemplar_controlling_for_everything(glmer8)

As time passes, the memory for the association decays and recombined responses increase relative to old responses. However, up until 50 trials past there is no increase in new responses, which means that memory for the items is intact. Afterwards memory for the items starts to decay as well.

7) Response times as a function of number of previous exemplars of the relations

Because response times are heavily influenced by the trial number (people get faster as the experiment progresses), all plots below show response times after taking into account the effect of trial number. For recombined response people get slower to respond correctly the more previous exemplars there were of the relation. For old responses there is no change.

plot_rt_by_trialtype_nexemp_controlling_for_trial(df1)

Even more interestingly, if we look at RTs depending on how long ago the previous exemplar of the relation was, we see that for old responses, people get slower as time since the previous occurence increases, due to memory decay and forgetting the association. However, recombined responses, people get faster, because as the previous exemplar memory decays more and more, it has less influence on the current one (Not really obvious from the figure, but both slopes are significant).

plot_rt_by_distance_to_previous_controlling_for_all(df1)
